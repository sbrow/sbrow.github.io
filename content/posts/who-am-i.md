I think you can tell a lot about a person from their failures, (or lack thereof);
and as a man who is *phenomenal* at being hard on himself, I've racked up quite
a few in my time:

- I am a college dropout.
- I co-produced a card game, but didn't see it through to the bitter end.
- I was a seasonal worker at Best Buy, but was dropped after the season ended.
- I struggled to get by as a freelance developer, until I botched a job so badly
that I was offered full-time work. (Yes, you heard that right)

In short, I am a self-taught, Full-Stack (?) :shrug: developer, trying to find
his way in a busy, difficult industry with naught but Google (and now ChatGPT),
by my side.

This blog is my frantic attempt to keep my sanity, grow my confidence, and
hopefully help others who are struggling to find guidance in this industry.

As a solo developer, both at work and in my tiny friend group, I am constantly
battling [Imposter Syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impostor_syndrome).
I have no one to turn to for advice, no one to bounce ideas off of, and no one
to endorse my architecture and design decisions.

But before the pity comments start rolling in, let me state that although this
post may have started off pretty glum, I am merely providing context for the
circumstances that brought me here. I'm not here to complain about first-world
problems or attempt to have some sort of "Who's the most miserable" pissing
contest- I'm here to share my experiences, and in doing so, help myself and
anyone bored enough to read this blog.

## Why I'm Here

I'll be honest, what truly inspired me to start this blog was when [Theo.gg](https://t3.gg/)
rolled into my YouTube feed. While I don't mean to throw shade, his attitude
is one that definitely felt like the trap I fell into when I started in this
industry. Everything was React, SPAs and JavaScript. Don't get me wrong, I like
JavaScript. Despite being a full-time Laravel Developer for most of my professional
career, I am 100% a JS Dev at heart. If I ever end up in a real interview and
have to code on the fly, I will be spinning up a fresh bun environment and doing
[TDD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test-driven_development).

However, when it comes to deploying real world apps...

That's not to say that
those tools are bad, but everyone's always excited about the latest JS
framework, and sometimes productive, (albeit less exciting) tools get lost
in the weeds.

In particular, his
video [The Javascript Framework Tier List](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WJRf7dh5Zw)
really rubbed me the wrong way. Yes I'm **fully aware** that he stated at the
beginning that it was mostly for laughs, and shouldn't be taken seriously,
but statements such as **\<Insert actual quote here>** "ugh, they're putting
JavaScript directly in HTML attributes" and "HTMX has made Alpine.js obsolete"
Feed into the narrative that things like Next.js and Astro are the future, and
Full-Stack tools like Ruby on Rails and Laravel are the past. I think the truth
is that both tools have their place, and acting like JSX is the only way to build
web pages will lead you away from many good options that may work better for you.

The old guard still has value, and I think that dismissing that out of hand is a
mistake. I want people to know that you don't have to follow the latest trends to
be a successful and productive developer.

> The best editor, framework, or programming language is the one that makes you the most productive.
